Chapter Two

The Brewing Process

All-grain brewing gives you complete control. These are the critical stages where intermediate brewers level up.

Mash Temperature & Efficiency

Mash at 64–67°C for a drier, more fermentable wort. Push to 68–70°C for a fuller body with residual sweetness. Your mash efficiency targets 70–80% — track it every batch to dial in your system.

75% Target Efficiency
67°C Sweet Spot Mash
60 min Standard Mash
90 min High-Adjunct Mash

Boil & Hop Additions

A vigorous rolling boil sterilizes wort and drives off DMS (dimethyl sulfide — a cooked corn off-flavor). Precise hop timing is everything: bittering additions at 60 minutes, flavor at 15, aroma at flameout or whirlpool. Whirlpool hops at 77°C extract oils with minimal bitterness.

Fermentation Control

Temperature is king. Even 1°C above optimal yeast range can push esters and fusel alcohols. A simple fermentation chamber (a chest freezer with an inkbird controller) is the best upgrade an intermediate brewer can make. Pitch the right amount of yeast — underpitching is the root of half all off-flavors.

Key Insight Calculate your pitch rate: for ales, target 0.75 million cells/mL/°Plato. For lagers, double it. Use an online pitch rate calculator — it takes two minutes and eliminates one of the biggest failure points.
Chapter Three

Field-Tested Recipes

Three recipes built for the intermediate brewer — each targeting a different technique and style. All scaled to 19L (5 US gallons), targeting 75% efficiency.You can find more recipes like this on byo.com

West Coast IPA

Clean, resinous, bone-dry. The classic benchmark.

OG 1.065 IBU 65 SRM 6 ABV ~7%

Grain Bill

  • 2-Row Pale Malt 4.5 kg
  • Carapils (Dextrine) 225 g
  • Crystal 40L 225 g
  • White Wheat Malt 225 g

Hops

  • Magnum (60 min) 28 g
  • Centennial (15 min) 21 g
  • Simcoe (Flameout) 28 g
  • Citra (Dry Hop 4d) 42 g
  • Mosaic (Dry Hop 4d) 42 g

Yeast

  • WY1056 / US-05 1 pkg

Process Notes

  1. Mash at 66°C for 60 min for a dry finish.
  2. Collect 26 liters pre-boil. Boil 60 min vigorously.
  3. Chill to 19°C. Pitch yeast, ferment 5–7 days.
  4. Dry hop when gravity reaches 1.020, for 4 days at 20°C.
  5. Cold crash to 1°C for 48 hours before packaging.
  6. Carbonate to 2.4 vol CO₂. Drink fresh within 60 days.
Tip Add dry hops under CO₂ pressure to minimize oxygen pickup. Oxygen is the enemy of hoppy beers.

Export Stout

Rich and roasty, with a silky body and restrained bitterness.

OG 1.072 IBU 40 SRM 40 ABV ~7.5%

Grain Bill

  • Maris Otter 5.0 kg
  • Flaked Oats 450 g
  • Chocolate Malt 340 g
  • Roasted Barley 225 g
  • Crystal 80L 225 g
  • Black Patent Malt 115 g

Hops

  • East Kent Goldings 60 min 42 g
  • East Kent Goldings 15 min 14 g

Yeast

  • WY1084 Irish Ale 1 pkg

Process Notes

  1. Mash at 68°C for 60 min — a fuller body complements the roast.
  2. Sparge slowly. Pre-boil 28 liters. Boil 90 min to reduce DMS risk.
  3. Chill to 18°C. Ferment for 10–14 days.
  4. Allow diacetyl rest at 20°C for 48 hours after primary is complete.
  5. Cold condition for 1 week at 1°C for smoothness.
  6. Carbonate to 2.0 vol CO₂ — traditional stout level.
Tip Add 5–10% of your roasted grains post-mash (cold-steep method) to reduce harsh acridness while preserving color.

Bavarian Hefeweizen

Banana and clove driven. A masterclass in yeast control.

OG 1.052 IBU 14 SRM 4 ABV ~5.3%

Grain Bill

  • White Wheat Malt 3.2 kg
  • Pilsner Malt 2.3 kg
  • Rice Hulls (lautering) 225 g

Hops

  • Hallertau (60 min) 34 g

Yeast

  • WY3068 Weihenstephan 2 pkgs

Process Notes

  1. Perform a ferulic acid rest at 44°C for 15 min before mashing — this boosts clove character (4-vinylguaiacol).
  2. Mash at 67°C for 45 min.
  3. Ferment at 17°C to favor banana esters. Raise to 18°C at terminal gravity.
  4. Do NOT fine, filter, or cold crash — haze is correct here.
  5. Package with some yeast in suspension. Serve at 7–10°C.
Tip WY3068 is incredibly temperature-sensitive. At 18°C+ you get banana. Below 16°C you get clove. Most great Hefeweizens sit right at 16°C.
Chapter Four

Advanced Techniques

These are the methods that separate consistently excellent brewers from occasional lucky ones. Each represents a genuine upgrade in your brewing capability.

Repitching harvested yeast is one of the most cost-effective skills to develop. After fermentation, cold crash to compact the yeast cake, then decant the beer. Transfer healthy slurry into sanitized mason jars — store at 1°C and use within 2 weeks.

Avoid harvesting from heavily dry-hopped beers or any batch you suspect had infection issues. After 5–6 generations the yeast profile may drift — track generations in a brewing log. The first two or three repitches often yield better performance than fresh packets, as the yeast is already adapted to your wort.

Oxygen pickup during dry hopping is the number one destroyer of hoppy beers. Even a few ppb of dissolved oxygen will cause trans-2-nonenal — cardboard aroma — within days of packaging.

The solution: use a closed fermentor with a floating dip tube (or a pressure-capable vessel like a Speidel or Fermzilla). Purge all headspace with CO₂ before opening. Add hops via a hop torpedo or sealed port while maintaining positive pressure. This technique alone can extend your IPA's peak freshness from 3 weeks to 6–8 weeks.

Modern well-modified malts rarely require step mashing, but it's a powerful tool for specific styles. The key rests: 40°C for ferulic acid (clove in Hefeweizens), 45°C for beta-glucan reduction (improves lautering with adjuncts), 60–65°C for beta-amylase (fermentability), and 68–72°C for alpha-amylase (body).

If your system doesn't support direct-fire step mashing, use a HERMS or RIMS setup, or blend hot water additions (infusion step mashing). Protein rests at 50°C are rarely needed with modern malts and can actually harm head retention if overdone.

Target mash pH of 5.2–5.4 for maximum enzyme activity and clarity. Use lactic acid or acidulated malt to lower pH, and calcium carbonate (chalk) to raise it.

For mineral additions: calcium sulfate (gypsum) enhances hop bitterness perception and dryness — ideal for IPAs. Calcium chloride adds softness and malt roundness — ideal for stouts, milds, and Helles lagers. Aim for 50–100 ppm calcium at minimum for yeast health. Never exceed 400 ppm total sulfate. Brewing software like Bru'n Water or BrewFather makes this approachable in minutes.

For styles that demand clarity (lagers, Kölsch, Märzen), fining is essential. Irish moss or Whirlfloc at 15 minutes in the boil precipitates proteins during the cold break. Gelatin finings added to cold-crashed beer (1°C) bind yeast and drop it out within 48–72 hours.

Note: gelatin is not vegan. For vegan-friendly clarification, use Biofine Clear (silica hydrogel) or simply allow adequate cold conditioning time. Never fine a Hefeweizen, NEIPA, or any style where haze is intentional and style-appropriate.

Chapter Five

The Troubleshooter's Table

Identify off-flavors by what you taste or smell, and trace them back to root cause. Most problems in craft brewing are process problems, not ingredient problems.

What You Detect Root Cause Fix It Next Batch
Cooked corn / DMS Covered boil, inadequate boil vigor, or poor chill speed with Pilsner malt Uncovered kettle, rolling boil, chill under 27°C within 20 min
Butter / butterscotch Diacetyl — underpitching, premature cold crash, or weak yeast Diacetyl rest at 20°C for 2 days before crashing; pitch correct rate
Green apple / acetaldehyde Young beer packaged too early; yeast didn't fully clean up Allow extra 3–5 days at fermentation temp after apparent FG
Cardboard / paper Oxidation — either hot-side (boil) or cold-side (packaging) Minimize splashing, purge all vessels, dry hop under pressure
Astringency / harsh tannins Over-sparging, sparge water too hot (>77°C), or over-crushing malt Stop sparge at 1.010 gravity, limit sparge temp, check crush gap
Sour / vinegar Acetic acid bacteria (sanitation failure) or wild yeast contamination Re-audit sanitization procedure; replace old plastic equipment
Band-aid / medicinal Chlorophenols — chlorinated tap water reacting with wild yeast Use campden tablet (potassium metabisulfite) to neutralize chloramines
Thin body / watery Mash too low, over-attenuated, or insufficient specialty malt Mash at 68–69°C; add 5% oats or Crystal 60 to grain bill
Hazy when clarity expected Chill haze (protein-tannin), yeast in suspension, or starch haze Whirlfloc in boil + gelatin fining + extended cold crash at 1°C
Hot / boozy / solvent Fusel alcohols from fermenting too warm or severely underpitching Control fermentation temp strictly; pitch to correct cell count
The Golden Rule Change one variable per batch. When you troubleshoot, isolate. If you adjust mash temp, yeast pitch rate, and fermentation temperature simultaneously, you'll never know which variable fixed the problem — or created a new one.
